# Coaching Principles Framework

## Technical execution

### Behavior before outcome

- Explanation: Evaluate the controllable action first: readiness, footwork, spacing, decision, communication, support, and response after the play. Outcome still matters, but it is not the only proof of development.
- Why it matters: A good decision can fail because of execution or opponent quality; a poor decision can sometimes produce a goal. LaxHornet should help parents separate process from result.
- Applicable age groups: All youth ages; especially useful before high school when bodies and skill levels change quickly.
- Applicable positions: All positions.
- Relevant game phase: All phases.
- Observable positive behaviors: Attempts a correct next play under pressure; Keeps spacing/support even away from the ball; Recovers immediately after a turnover
- Observable warning signs: Only celebrated when scoring; Good outcomes from rushed or low-percentage choices; Player stops engaging after an error
- Statistics/events that could support the conclusion: Smart Play; Hustle Play; Assist; Turnover; Failed Clear; tags: good decision unsuccessful execution
- Cannot reliably be determined from statistics alone: Body language, coach instruction, opponent pressure quality, and whether a missed pass was caused by passer, receiver, or field conditions.
- Suggested player feedback: The result was not perfect, but the decision was the right one. Keep trusting that read and clean up the execution.
- Suggested development focus: Track one repeatable behavior next game, such as first clean pass after winning possession.
- Confidence: High

## Communication

### The ride home should protect learning

- Explanation: Post-game feedback should begin with support, curiosity, and one simple learning point, not a lecture or replay of every error.
- Why it matters: LaxHornet's recap should help parents encourage development without turning the app into a pressure device.
- Applicable age groups: All youth ages.
- Applicable positions: All positions.
- Relevant game phase: Postgame review.
- Observable positive behaviors: Parent asks player what they noticed; One next focus is chosen; Effort and learning are praised
- Observable warning signs: Too many corrections; Parent fixates on outcome; App becomes a criticism checklist
- Statistics/events that could support the conclusion: Family Recap; Talk About the Game prompts; Next Game Focus
- Cannot reliably be determined from statistics alone: Family tone, player readiness to talk, emotional state after the game.
- Suggested player feedback: Ask one question first: what play felt best today?
- Suggested development focus: Limit postgame discussion to one encouragement and one next focus.
- Confidence: High
- Supporting articles: [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/the-ride-home/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/i-love-watching-you-play/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/open-letter-dad-wont-stop-yelling/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/coaches-stop-dealing-parents-start-engaging/)


## Effort and competitiveness

### Competing is not just working hard

- Explanation: Effort becomes competitive when it is directed toward winning the next playable moment: loose ball, ride, recover, support, communicate, or make the next simple play.
- Why it matters: Raw hustle counts are helpful, but competitive quality is better captured through second effort, recovery, and pressure-response tags.
- Applicable age groups: All youth ages; expectations should be age-appropriate.
- Applicable positions: All positions.
- Relevant game phase: Loose balls, transition, rides, clears, late-game moments.
- Observable positive behaviors: Wins second effort; Turns a mistake into a recovery play; Stays involved away from the ball
- Observable warning signs: Runs hard without purpose; Stops after first failed attempt; Competes only when the ball is nearby
- Statistics/events that could support the conclusion: Hustle Play; Ground Ball; Backed Up Shot; Caused Turnover; tags: won second effort, recovered after mistake
- Cannot reliably be determined from statistics alone: Whether the player was following assignment, fatigue level, and sideline instruction.
- Suggested player feedback: That was a competitive play because you stayed in it after the first moment and helped the team get another chance.
- Suggested development focus: Win one second-effort play in each half.
- Confidence: High
- Supporting articles: [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/do-you-work-hard-or-do-you-compete/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/the-will-to-compete/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/can-youth-sports-fun-competitive/)

## Emotional response

### Competence builds confidence

- Explanation: Confidence is more durable when it grows from repeated, visible competence rather than praise alone.
- Why it matters: The app should use evidence-based encouragement: 'you did this three times' instead of empty reassurance.
- Applicable age groups: All youth ages.
- Applicable positions: All positions.
- Relevant game phase: Practice and games.
- Observable positive behaviors: Repeats a skill across games; Shows growth in a focus area; Attempts harder plays after success
- Observable warning signs: Confidence depends only on goals or wins; Player avoids actions after one failed attempt
- Statistics/events that could support the conclusion: Saved Next Game Focus completion; Smart Plays; Successful Clears; SOG % trend; GB trend
- Cannot reliably be determined from statistics alone: Self-talk, anxiety, and whether the player feels prepared.
- Suggested player feedback: Your confidence has something real behind it: you are repeating the play that used to be hard.
- Suggested development focus: Choose a visible skill that can be repeated three times next game.
- Confidence: High
- Supporting articles: [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/confidence-is-a-feeling-competence-is-a-behavior/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/confidence-is-earned/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/confidence-cannot-be-given-it-has-to-be-earned/)


### Safety and respect are prerequisites

- Explanation: Development feedback must not normalize bullying, humiliation, or abusive coaching. The app should encourage supportive adult behavior.
- Why it matters: Youth sports tools can increase pressure if they are framed as surveillance or blame. LaxHornet should stay development-first.
- Applicable age groups: All youth ages.
- Applicable positions: All positions.
- Relevant game phase: All adult-player interactions.
- Observable positive behaviors: Specific constructive feedback; Player feels safe to try; Adults model composure
- Observable warning signs: Shaming language; Stats used as punishment; Fear-based play
- Statistics/events that could support the conclusion: None reliable as proof; only indirect signs like avoidance or repeated low involvement
- Cannot reliably be determined from statistics alone: Emotional safety cannot be established from box-score data.
- Suggested player feedback: Use the data to support learning, not to label or blame.
- Suggested development focus: One encouraging observation before any correction.
- Confidence: High
- Supporting articles: [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/no-excuse-for-the-abuse/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/abusive-coaching-tolerated-sports/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/is-your-kids-coach-a-bully/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/the-accountability-problem-in-youth-sports/)


## Practice habits

### Learning environment changes performance

- Explanation: Athletes improve faster when they feel safe enough to try, fail, ask questions, and receive clear feedback.
- Why it matters: Feedback rules should avoid harsh labels and should never imply coach-grade certainty from parent-entered stats.
- Applicable age groups: All youth ages.
- Applicable positions: All positions.
- Relevant game phase: Practice, games, review.
- Observable positive behaviors: Tries a coached skill; Asks/answers questions; Bounces back from mistakes
- Observable warning signs: Avoids risk; Freezes after mistakes; Only plays safely to avoid criticism
- Statistics/events that could support the conclusion: Smart Play; Good Decision Unsuccessful Execution; Recovered After Mistake
- Cannot reliably be determined from statistics alone: Coach tone, psychological safety, player anxiety.
- Suggested player feedback: Trying the right play is part of learning. Keep the read; now sharpen the execution.
- Suggested development focus: Attempt the focus behavior before evaluating the outcome.
- Confidence: High
- Supporting articles: [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/creating-the-ideal-learning-environment/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/the-secret-ingredient-of-great-coaching/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/tough-love-in-coaching/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/the-one-question-coaches-ask-athletes/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/great-coaches-are-both-and-not-either-or/)


## Leadership

### Service shows up as team-first behavior

- Explanation: Strong teammates ask what they can give: support, communication, extra effort, emotional steadiness, and simple plays that help others.
- Why it matters: LaxHornet can identify and reward contribution beyond goals without pretending to measure character completely.
- Applicable age groups: U12 and older most directly; simplified for younger players.
- Applicable positions: All positions.
- Relevant game phase: Off-ball, transition, bench/team culture, unsettled moments.
- Observable positive behaviors: Supports ball carrier; Communicates early; Makes extra pass; Settles team after chaos
- Observable warning signs: Only engages when personally attacking; Forces plays to chase stats; Body language hurts teammates
- Statistics/events that could support the conclusion: Assist; Smart Play; Successful Clear; tags: supported ball carrier, extra pass, communicated early
- Cannot reliably be determined from statistics alone: Tone, teammate trust, bench behavior, and whether communication was helpful.
- Suggested player feedback: You helped the play even when you were not the finisher. That is the kind of contribution teams need.
- Suggested development focus: Add one support action before asking for the ball.
- Confidence: Medium-High
- Supporting articles: [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/the-one-quality-great-teammates-have-in-common/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/be-a-thermostat-not-a-thermometer-sample-chapter-from-our-new-book-the-champion-teammate/), [source](https://changingthegameproject.com/a-higher-purpose-than-winning/)


## Long-term player development

### Long-term development beats early labeling

- Explanation: Avoid fixed labels and early selection assumptions. Young athletes develop unevenly and need broad, enjoyable, varied experiences.
- Why it matters: LaxHornet should use temporary profiles and development stories, not permanent judgments.
- Applicable age groups: All youth ages; strongest for pre-high school.
- Applicable positions: All positions.
- Relevant game phase: Season review, roster/player profiles.
- Observable positive behaviors: Multiple contribution types; Growth in role over time; Willingness to try new skills
- Observable warning signs: Permanent labels from small samples; Over-specialization pressure; Only valuing early-maturing players
- Statistics/events that could support the conclusion: Season Player Profile; trend lines; multi-category contribution
- Cannot reliably be determined from statistics alone: Maturation timing, training history, motivation, multi-sport context.
- Suggested player feedback: This profile describes the current pattern, not who you are forever.
- Suggested development focus: Choose one skill growth target while preserving broad involvement.
- Confidence: High
